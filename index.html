Welcome to Systems Thinking for Civil Service Leaders

About This Resource Pack

This interactive teaching resource is designed for middle to senior level civil servants who want to understand and apply systems thinking in their leadership roles. The materials are practical, accessible, and focused on developing mindsets rather than completing technical exercises.

How to Use This Resource

This pack is structured as both a self-directed learning tool and a teaching resource for workshops and training sessions. Each section builds upon previous concepts while remaining accessible as standalone modules.

Navigation

  • Foundations: Core concepts and principles of systems thinking
  • Skill Development: Practical approaches to developing systems thinking capabilities

What is Systems Thinking?

Systems thinking is a way of understanding and working with complexity that recognises the interconnected nature of challenges facing government. Rather than focusing solely on individual problems or isolated solutions, systems thinking looks at the relationships, patterns, and structures that create the outcomes we observe.

In the Civil Service Context

This means recognising that policy challenges rarely exist in isolation. Housing shortages connect to transport planning, education outcomes, health inequalities, and economic development. Climate change intersects with agriculture, urban planning, public health, and social justice.

Systems vs Linear Thinking

Linear Thinking Asks:

  • What's the problem?
  • What caused it?
  • How do we fix it?
  • Who's responsible?

Systems Thinking Asks:

  • What patterns do we see over time?
  • What relationships create these outcomes?
  • How might solutions interact with the broader system?
  • What role do we play in creating current results?

Why Systems Thinking Matters

Traditional Approaches Often Fail Because They:

  • Address symptoms rather than root causes
  • Create unintended consequences
  • Generate short-term fixes that worsen long-term problems
  • Waste resources through duplicated efforts
  • Miss opportunities for integrated solutions

Systems Thinking Enables You To:

  • See the bigger picture while understanding your role within it
  • Anticipate unintended consequences before they occur
  • Find leverage points where small changes create large improvements
  • Build coalitions across departments and sectors
  • Design policies that work with, rather than against, system dynamics

Real-World Examples

Housing Policy Example

Linear approach: Build more houses to solve housing shortage.

Systems approach: Consider how housing connects to transport, schools, employment, infrastructure capacity, and community services. Recognise that simply building houses without considering these connections might create new problems.

Skills Training Example

Linear approach: Provide more training courses to address skills gaps.

Systems approach: Examine why skills gaps persist despite existing training. Consider employer needs, training quality, accessibility, career pathways, and economic incentives that might discourage skill development.

Key Principles of Systems Thinking

🔗 Interconnectedness

Everything is connected to everything else. Changes in one part of the system ripple through to other parts, often in unexpected ways.

🎯 Purpose

Every system exists for a purpose, though this may not always be explicit. Sometimes the real purpose differs from the stated purpose.

🏗️ Structure Drives Behaviour

How a system is organised determines what it produces. If you want different outcomes, you often need to change the structure.

🧠 Mental Models Matter

Our assumptions and beliefs shape what we see and how we act. Changing outcomes often requires changing how we think about the system.

⚖️ Leverage Points Exist

Small changes in the right place can produce significant improvements. The art is finding where to push.

💡 Practical Application

When facing any challenge, ask yourself: "What principle might be most relevant here?" This helps shift from reactive problem-solving to thoughtful system intervention.

Expanding Your Perspective

Practice Asking Different Questions

Instead of asking...

  • "How do we solve this problem?"
  • "Who is to blame?"
  • "What's the quick fix?"

Try asking...

  • "What is this problem a symptom of?"
  • "What about our system produces this outcome?"
  • "What would a sustainable solution look like?"

Develop Pattern Recognition

Start noticing patterns in your daily work:

  • Look for recurring themes across different policy areas
  • Notice when the same issues keep arising despite repeated interventions
  • Identify situations where actions create reactions that either reinforce or balance the original action

Pattern Recognition in Practice

Example: If multiple departments keep reporting similar implementation challenges, this might indicate a systemic issue with how policies are designed or communicated, rather than individual departmental failures.

Zoom In and Zoom Out

Practice shifting between different levels of perspective:

  • Zoom in: What's happening right here, right now?
  • Zoom out: What patterns emerge over time?
  • Zoom further out: What structures and systems create these patterns?

Thinking in Connections

Broaden Your View of Who Matters

When facing any policy issue, naturally extend your thinking beyond the obvious players. Who else might be affected? Who has informal influence? Often the most important relationships aren't the ones on the organisational chart.

💡 Quick Practice

For your current project, spend two minutes listing everyone who might be affected by or could influence the outcome. Include people outside government, informal influencers, and those who might be affected indirectly.

Follow the Ripple Effects

Get into the habit of asking "and then what happens?" When you're considering a policy change, mentally trace through how it might play out. What might it trigger in other departments? How might people respond in ways you didn't anticipate?

Example: Ripple Effect Thinking

Policy: Increase minimum wage

Direct effect: Workers earn more money

And then what happens?

  • Some businesses might reduce hours or staff
  • Others might raise prices
  • Some might invest in automation
  • Consumer spending might change
  • Demand for social services might shift

The insight: Supporting low-wage workers might require coordinated policies across employment, business support, and social services.

Notice When Things Push Back

Pay attention to patterns where good intentions meet resistance or create unexpected results. Sometimes this resistance tells you something important about how the system really works, not how you think it should work.

When change efforts keep hitting the same obstacles, the obstacle might be the message about what needs to change in the system structure.

Challenging Mental Models

Examine Your Assumptions

We all carry assumptions about how things work. These mental models shape what we notice, how we interpret events, and what solutions we consider.

Questions for Self-Reflection:

  • What beliefs do I hold about how government works?
  • Where did these beliefs come from?
  • What evidence might challenge these assumptions?
  • What am I not seeing because of my current perspective?

⚠️ Common Mental Model Traps

  • "People resist change" → Maybe they resist poorly designed change
  • "We need better communication" → Maybe we need better listening
  • "More data will solve this" → Maybe we need better sense-making
  • "Someone should be accountable" → Maybe the system makes success difficult

Seek Diverse Perspectives

Actively look for viewpoints that challenge your current thinking:

  • Regularly engage with stakeholders from different backgrounds
  • Listen to frontline staff who see system effects directly
  • Include voices that are often marginalised in policy discussions
  • Ask people to disagree with you constructively

Test Your Thinking

Build habits that help you question your assumptions:

  • Use "what if" scenarios to stress-test your understanding
  • Look for disconfirming evidence
  • Ask "What would have to be true for this opposite view to make sense?"
  • Be willing to change your mind when new information emerges

Attributes of Systems Leaders

🔍 Systems Awareness

  • Sees connections between different policy areas and organisational functions
  • Understands emergence - how system behaviour arises from interactions between parts
  • Recognises patterns across time and different contexts
  • Appreciates complexity without being overwhelmed by it

🤝 Collaborative Leadership

  • Works across boundaries - departmental, sectoral, and hierarchical
  • Builds networks of relationships that enable collective action
  • Facilitates dialogue between different perspectives and interests
  • Shares power appropriately to enable system-wide solutions

🎯 Adaptive Capacity

  • Comfortable with uncertainty and ambiguous situations
  • Learns from failure and treats setbacks as learning opportunities
  • Experiments safely with small-scale pilots before full implementation
  • Adjusts course based on feedback and changing circumstances

⏳ Long-term Orientation

  • Balances short and long-term thinking in decision-making
  • Invests in system health even when benefits aren't immediately visible
  • Considers intergenerational impacts of policy decisions
  • Builds sustainable solutions rather than quick fixes

🪞 Reflective Practice

  • Regularly examines their own role in creating system outcomes
  • Seeks feedback actively from multiple sources
  • Questions assumptions and mental models
  • Commits to continuous learning and development

Practical Development Steps

Start with Self-Awareness

  1. Reflect on your current approach: How do you typically tackle complex problems?
  2. Identify your blind spots: What perspectives or stakeholders do you consistently overlook?
  3. Examine your mental models: What assumptions guide your thinking about how government works?

💡 Self-Assessment Questions

  • When facing a problem, do I immediately look for someone to blame or something to fix?
  • Do I regularly seek out perspectives that challenge my thinking?
  • Am I comfortable not having all the answers?
  • Do I consider how my actions might create unintended consequences?

Expand Your Learning

  • Read broadly beyond your immediate policy area
  • Attend cross-departmental meetings and forums
  • Engage with academic research and think tanks working on systems approaches
  • Learn from other sectors - what can business, NGOs, or international examples teach?

Practice Systems Approaches

  • Start small with low-stakes situations to practice systems thinking
  • Choose one complex issue you're working on and spend time understanding all the connections
  • Run a pre-mortem on a policy proposal - what could go wrong and why?
  • Design small experiments to test assumptions before full implementation

Build Your Network

  • Cultivate relationships across departments and sectors
  • Create informal learning groups with peers interested in systems approaches
  • Mentor others in systems thinking approaches
  • Engage with external stakeholders regularly to understand system effects

Embed Systems Thinking in Your Work

  • Use systems questions in meetings and policy discussions
  • Advocate for cross-departmental solutions when appropriate
  • Include systems thinking in team development and training
  • Measure system health alongside specific outcomes

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

⚠️ Analysis Paralysis

Don't let the complexity prevent action - start somewhere and learn as you go. Perfect understanding isn't required before taking action.

⚠️ Silver Bullet Thinking

Resist the urge to find the one perfect solution - sustainable change usually requires multiple interventions working together over time.

⚠️ Ignoring Culture and Politics

Technical solutions that don't account for human behaviour and political realities often fail. Systems include people, and people have emotions, interests, and motivations.

⚠️ Working in Isolation

Systems change requires collective action - you can't do it alone. Build coalitions and partnerships early in the process.

⚠️ Impatience

Systems change takes time - maintain focus on long-term transformation while delivering short-term progress. Celebrate small wins along the way.

How to Avoid These Pitfalls

  • Set learning goals, not just outcome goals
  • Start with small experiments that test key assumptions
  • Engage stakeholders in designing solutions, not just implementing them
  • Build in regular review points to adjust course based on what you're learning
  • Communicate progress in ways that maintain momentum and support

Learning Activities

Individual Reflection Activities

Activity 1: Systems Stories (15 minutes)

Purpose: Develop pattern recognition and systems awareness

Instructions: Think of a recurring problem in your organisation. Write a brief story describing not just what happens, but the patterns you notice over time. What keeps the problem coming back?

Debrief: What does this pattern tell you about the underlying system?

Activity 2: Ripple Effect Mapping (20 minutes)

Purpose: Practice thinking in connections

Instructions: Choose a policy change you're currently considering. Write it in the centre of a page. Around it, write all the potential effects you can think of - direct and indirect, intended and unintended. Draw lines showing connections.

Debrief: Which effects surprised you? What connections hadn't you considered before?

Activity 3: Assumption Testing (10 minutes)

Purpose: Challenge mental models

Instructions: Write down three assumptions you hold about a current challenge. For each assumption, write: "What if the opposite were true?" Explore what that would mean.

Debrief: How might these alternative perspectives change your approach?

Group Discussion Activities

Group Activity 1: Systems Café (45 minutes)

Setup: Small groups rotate through different tables, each focused on a different systems principle

Process:

  • Table 1: Interconnectedness - How are our current challenges connected?
  • Table 2: Purpose - What is our system actually designed to achieve?
  • Table 3: Structure - What structures might be driving current outcomes?
  • Table 4: Mental Models - What assumptions might be limiting our thinking?
  • Table 5: Leverage Points - Where might small changes have big impacts?

Debrief: What insights emerged across tables? What patterns did you notice?

Group Activity 2: Perspective Taking (30 minutes)

Purpose: Expand perspective and challenge assumptions

Process:

  1. Present a policy challenge to the group
  2. Assign different stakeholder perspectives to small groups
  3. Each group discusses the challenge from their assigned perspective
  4. Groups present their perspective to others
  5. Full group discusses what they learned

Debrief: What perspectives were missing from our usual discussions? How might this change our approach?

Case Study Exercises

Case Study Template: Systems Analysis

Step 1: Present the challenge or policy issue

Step 2: Map the key stakeholders and their relationships

Step 3: Identify the patterns and recurring themes

Step 4: Explore underlying structures and mental models

Step 5: Identify potential leverage points

Step 6: Consider unintended consequences of interventions

Debrief: How does this analysis compare to traditional approaches? What new insights emerged?

Discussion Points for Workshops

Opening Discussion Questions

  • Think of a time when a good intention led to an unexpected outcome. What happened and why?
  • What's an example of a problem that keeps coming back despite repeated attempts to fix it?
  • When have you seen a small change create a disproportionately large impact?
  • What's a policy area where different departments seem to be working at cross-purposes?

Mid-Session Discussion Points

On Interconnectedness:

  • How do housing policies affect education outcomes?
  • What connections do you see between your department's work and others?
  • When has a solution in one area created problems elsewhere?

On Mental Models:

  • What assumptions do we make about why people behave as they do?
  • How might our organisational culture shape what we see as possible?
  • What would someone from outside government see that we might miss?

On Systems Leadership:

  • What does leadership look like when you can't control all the variables?
  • How do you influence a system you're part of?
  • When is it appropriate to slow down and when to speed up?

Challenging Questions for Advanced Groups

  • How might our current performance measurement systems encourage the problems we're trying to solve?
  • What would it mean to design policies that get better over time rather than delivering fixed outcomes?
  • How do we balance accountability with the recognition that outcomes emerge from complex interactions?
  • What if the "beneficiaries" of our policies were the primary designers of solutions?

Closing Reflection Questions

  • What's one assumption you're willing to question after today's discussion?
  • Where do you see the biggest opportunity to apply systems thinking in your current work?
  • What would you need to do differently as a leader to work more systemically?
  • Who would you need to have different conversations with to understand your system better?

Reflection Questions for Development

Self-Assessment Questions

Systems Awareness

  • Do I regularly consider how my actions might affect other parts of the organisation or system?
  • When problems recur, do I look for patterns or focus on individual incidents?
  • Am I curious about why systems produce their current outcomes?
  • Do I see connections between seemingly separate issues?

Collaborative Leadership

  • Do I actively seek out perspectives different from my own?
  • Am I comfortable sharing power and control to achieve better outcomes?
  • Do I build relationships across organisational boundaries?
  • Can I facilitate productive conversations between people with conflicting views?

Adaptive Capacity

  • How comfortable am I with ambiguity and uncertainty?
  • Do I treat failures as learning opportunities or problems to avoid?
  • Am I willing to experiment with new approaches?
  • Do I adjust my approach based on feedback and results?

Long-term Orientation

  • Do I consider the long-term consequences of short-term decisions?
  • Am I willing to invest time in building system capacity even when benefits aren't immediate?
  • Do I think about how current decisions might affect future generations?
  • Can I balance urgency with sustainability?

Reflective Practice

  • Do I regularly examine my own role in creating the outcomes I see?
  • Am I open to feedback that challenges my approach?
  • Do I question my own assumptions and mental models?
  • Am I committed to continuous learning and development?

Deeper Reflection Questions

Weekly Reflection

  • What patterns did I notice in my work this week?
  • Where did I see evidence of interconnectedness?
  • What assumptions guided my decisions?
  • Who did I not include in important conversations?
  • What feedback did the system give me about my actions?

Monthly Review

  • What systems patterns am I beginning to recognize in my area of work?
  • How has my understanding of key challenges evolved?
  • What new relationships have I built and why do they matter?
  • Where have I seen small changes create larger impacts?
  • What do I need to learn more about to be more effective?

Quarterly Deep Dive

  • How has my role in the system changed over the past quarter?
  • What mental models have I challenged or changed?
  • Where have I been most and least successful in working systemically?
  • What would colleagues say about how I've developed as a systems leader?
  • What's my next learning edge in systems thinking?

Team Reflection Questions

For Team Meetings

  • What patterns are we seeing across our different work streams?
  • Where are we working in silos when we should be connecting?
  • What assumptions are guiding our team's approach?
  • How are our actions affecting other parts of the organisation?
  • What feedback is the system giving us about our effectiveness?

For Project Reviews

  • What did we learn about the system through this project?
  • Where did we see unintended consequences?
  • What connections did we discover that we hadn't anticipated?
  • How did our mental models change through this work?
  • What would we do differently with our current understanding?

Making Reflection Practical

Start small: Choose just one or two questions that resonate with you and use them consistently.

Make it routine: Build reflection into existing meetings and processes rather than creating separate activities.

Share insights: Discussing your reflections with trusted colleagues often deepens the learning.

Focus on learning: The goal is insight and growth, not perfect answers.
